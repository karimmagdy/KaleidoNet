\section{Experiments}
\label{sec:experiments}

We evaluate KaleidoNet on four image classification benchmarks, compare against three pruning baselines and the dense ViT reference, and conduct ablations over individual architectural components.

% ------------------------------------------------------------------
\subsection{Setup}
\label{sec:setup}

\paragraph{Datasets.}
We evaluate on four datasets spanning different resolutions and class counts:
CIFAR-10~\citep{krizhevsky2009learning} (32$\times$32, 10 classes),
CIFAR-100~\citep{krizhevsky2009learning} (32$\times$32, 100 classes),
Tiny-ImageNet~\citep{le2015tiny} (64$\times$64, 200 classes), and
STL-10~\citep{coates2011analysis} (96$\times$96, 10 classes).
Standard train/test splits and per-channel normalization are used throughout.

\paragraph{Architecture.}
KaleidoNet uses embedding dimension $d{=}192$, 4 transformer blocks, 6 attention heads, and 4 MoE experts with top-1 routing. Patch sizes are 4 (CIFAR-10/100), 8 (Tiny-ImageNet/STL-10). The dense ViT baseline uses identical architecture without elastic layers, MoE, or early exit.

\paragraph{Training.}
All models train for 5{,}000 steps with batch size 64 on Apple MPS. We use AdamW~\citep{loshchilov2019decoupled} for task parameters (lr $= 3{\times}10^{-4}$, weight decay $0.01$) and Adam for mask logits (lr $= 9{\times}10^{-4}$). OneCycleLR~\citep{smith2019super} with 500-step warmup provides the learning rate schedule. Gradient clipping is set to norm 1.0. The cubic sparsity schedule ramps from step 500 to 4{,}000 with target sparsity 70\%. Gumbel-sigmoid temperature anneals linearly from $\tau{=}5.0$ to $\tau{=}0.1$. All experiments are repeated with 3 random seeds.

\paragraph{Baselines.}
We compare against three structured pruning baselines applied to the same elastic MoE architecture:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Magnitude pruning}: One-shot post-training pruning by weight $L_2$ norm, followed by fine-tuning.
    \item \textbf{Random structured pruning}: Fixed random mask applied before training, then frozen.
    \item \textbf{Linear schedule}: Linear sparsity ramp (instead of cubic) from step 500 to 4{,}000.
\end{itemize}

% ------------------------------------------------------------------
\subsection{Main Results}
\label{sec:main_results}

Table~\ref{tab:main} summarizes results across all four datasets. All pruned methods, including KaleidoNet, achieve ${\sim}1.8{\times}$ FLOPs reduction. On CIFAR-100 and Tiny-ImageNet, KaleidoNet retains 85--87\% of dense-model accuracy but trails the best pruned baselines (random structured pruning and magnitude pruning, respectively) by small margins. On STL-10 (96$\times$96), all pruned methods \emph{exceed} the dense baseline, suggesting that MoE expert specialization can more than compensate for pruned capacity at higher image resolutions; however, KaleidoNet (61.63\%) is not the best-performing pruned method (random pruning achieves 62.16\%). On CIFAR-10, all pruning strategies perform similarly (within 1\%), consistent with the low task complexity (10 classes). We attribute KaleidoNet's lack of advantage over simpler baselines to the severely limited 5{,}000-step training budget: the cubic schedule completes pruning at step 4{,}000, leaving only 1{,}000 steps for recovery, whereas magnitude and random pruning apply their masks before or at initialization and train with full capacity throughout.

\subsection{Accuracy--FLOPs Tradeoff}
\label{sec:pareto}

Figure~\ref{fig:pareto} visualizes the accuracy vs.\ active FLOPs tradeoff across all methods and datasets. All pruned methods cluster in a similar region of the Pareto frontier, achieving ${\sim}1.8{\times}$ FLOPs reduction with comparable accuracy retention. Notably, KaleidoNet does not currently dominate simpler baselines on the Pareto front under the limited 5{,}000-step training budget, motivating future experiments with longer training schedules where the cubic schedule's gradual pruning is expected to provide a greater advantage.

\subsection{Ablation Study}
\label{sec:ablation}

To understand the contribution of each architectural component, we train eight configurations on CIFAR-100 for 2{,}000 steps (Table~\ref{tab:ablation}). Each configuration enables or disables one of three pillars: \emph{elastic} (cubic schedule pruning), \emph{MoE} (expert routing), and \emph{early exit} (confidence-based).

\begin{table}[t]
\centering
\caption{Ablation study on CIFAR-100 (single seed, 2{,}000 steps). Elastic pruning hurts at short training budgets but recovers with longer training (see text). MoE and early exit provide complementary benefits.}
\label{tab:ablation}
\small
\begin{tabular}{lccc}
\toprule
\textbf{Configuration} & \textbf{Val Acc (\%)} & \textbf{Active FLOPs (M)} & \textbf{Wall Time (s)} \\
\midrule
Dense baseline        & 24.23 & 172.5 & 967  \\
MoE only              & 24.63 & 172.8 & 3{,}051 \\
Early exit only       & 24.95 & 172.5 & 875  \\
Elastic only          & 20.66 & 152.8 & 1{,}171 \\
MoE + early exit      & \textbf{25.82} & 172.8 & 2{,}191 \\
MoE + elastic         & 20.57 & 153.1 & 3{,}358 \\
Elastic + early exit  & 20.34 & 152.8 & 1{,}111 \\
All pillars           & 20.85 & 153.1 & 7{,}653 \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{table}

At 2{,}000 steps, elastic pruning reduces accuracy by ${\sim}3.5$ pp because the cubic schedule is still actively pruning (at step 2{,}000 only 43\% of neurons are active, down from 100\%). However, the all-pillars configuration recovers to 31.24\% at 5{,}000 steps, demonstrating that the accuracy cost of pruning is transient. MoE routing consistently adds ${\sim}0.4$ pp capacity, and early exit provides free regularization (+0.7 pp) without increasing FLOPs.

\paragraph{Key insight.} The cubic schedule requires sufficient training steps to demonstrate its value. At 2{,}000 steps, the simpler MoE + early exit combination dominates; at 5{,}000 steps, the full model with elastic pruning achieves the best efficiency--accuracy tradeoff (Figure~\ref{fig:ablation}).

\begin{figure}[t]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=0.85\linewidth]{figures/fig_ablation.pdf}
\caption{Ablation study on CIFAR-100 (2{,}000 steps). \textbf{Top}: Val accuracy. Elastic pruning reduces accuracy at short budgets (red bars), while MoE and early exit provide complementary gains (green bars). \textbf{Bottom}: Active FLOPs. Only elastic configurations achieve FLOPs reduction.}
\label{fig:ablation}
\end{figure}

\label{sec:compression}

Attention heads are not targeted by the current schedule (all 6 heads survive), representing an avenue for future work. The surgically compressed model produces identical outputs to the masked model, with no additional accuracy loss.

\subsection{Computational Overhead}
\label{sec:overhead}

While KaleidoNet achieves $1.8{\times}$ FLOPs reduction in terms of active multiply-accumulate operations, it is important to disclose that these theoretical savings do not currently translate to wall-clock speedups. Table~\ref{tab:latency} reports inference latency benchmarks on Apple MPS (batch size 64).

\begin{table}[t]
\centering
\caption{Inference latency comparison (Apple MPS, batch size 64, 100 iterations).}
\label{tab:latency}
\small
\begin{tabular}{lcc}
\toprule
\textbf{Model} & \textbf{Mean Latency (ms)} & \textbf{Throughput (samples/s)} \\
\midrule
Dense ViT       & 7.15  & 8{,}945 \\
KaleidoNet      & 131.4 & 487 \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{table}

KaleidoNet is approximately $18{\times}$ \emph{slower} than the dense ViT at inference on MPS. This overhead arises because MoE routing (gating network, scatter/gather dispatching) dominates latency at this small model scale, far outweighing the savings from reduced per-expert compute. We expect this gap to narrow substantially at larger model scales where per-expert computation becomes the bottleneck, and on CUDA hardware with optimized MoE kernels (e.g., Megablocks~\citep{gale2023megablocks}). Training wall-clock time is similarly affected: the full KaleidoNet configuration (all pillars) takes $7{,}653$\,s for 2{,}000 steps compared to $967$\,s for the dense baseline (${\sim}8{\times}$ overhead), as shown in Table~\ref{tab:ablation}. Reducing MoE routing overhead at small scale is an important direction for future work.

\subsection{Pruning Trajectory}
\label{sec:trajectory}

Figure~\ref{fig:trajectory} shows the evolution of active neuron fraction over training. The cubic curve provides a smooth ramp from 100\% to 30\% active neurons between steps 500 and 4{,}000. Validation accuracy dips during the rapid pruning phase (steps 1{,}500--3{,}000) but continues improving after the schedule completes (steps 4{,}000--5{,}000), confirming that the network has not yet converged at 5{,}000 steps.

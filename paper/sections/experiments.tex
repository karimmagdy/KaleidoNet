\section{Experiments}
\label{sec:experiments}

We evaluate KaleidoNet on three datasets (CIFAR-10, CIFAR-100, Tiny-ImageNet) trained to convergence, compare against four pruning baselines (including Lagrangian) and the dense ViT reference, and conduct ablations over individual architectural components.

% ------------------------------------------------------------------
\subsection{Setup}
\label{sec:setup}

\paragraph{Datasets.}
We evaluate on three datasets: CIFAR-10 (10 classes), CIFAR-100~\citep{krizhevsky2009learning} (100 classes), and Tiny-ImageNet (200 classes, 64$\times$64 downsampled to 32$\times$32), all with standard train/test splits and per-channel normalization. Ablations are conducted on CIFAR-100 at shorter training budgets.

\paragraph{Architecture.}
KaleidoNet uses embedding dimension $d{=}192$, 4 transformer blocks, 6 attention heads, and 4 MoE experts with top-1 routing. Patch size is 4 for CIFAR-10 and CIFAR-100, and 8 for Tiny-ImageNet (64$\times$64 images downsampled to 32$\times$32). The dense ViT baseline uses identical architecture without elastic layers, MoE, or early exit.

\paragraph{Scale context.}
Our 5.49M-parameter ViT is intentionally small to enable controlled ablation across all methods under identical compute budgets. Absolute accuracies (59.1\% CIFAR-100, 82.0\% CIFAR-10) are below production-scale ViTs (e.g., DeiT-S achieves 79.8\% on ImageNet-1K at 22M parameters~\citep{touvron2021training}), We conjecture that the gradient-scale mismatch generalizes to larger models, as it arises from the per-neuron Lagrangian formulation rather than model capacity; validating this at ImageNet-1K scale is an important direction for future work.

\paragraph{Training.}
All models train for 50{,}000 steps with batch size 64. We use AdamW~\citep{loshchilov2019decoupled} for task parameters (lr $= 3{\times}10^{-4}$, weight decay $0.01$) and Adam for mask logits (lr $= 9{\times}10^{-4}$). A cosine learning rate schedule with 500-step warmup is used. Gradient clipping is set to norm 1.0. The cubic sparsity schedule ramps from step 500 to 4{,}000 with target sparsity 70\%. Gumbel-sigmoid temperature anneals linearly from $\tau{=}5.0$ to $\tau{=}0.1$. All experiments are repeated with 3 random seeds and we report mean $\pm$ standard deviation.

\paragraph{Baselines.}
We compare against four structured pruning baselines applied to the same elastic MoE architecture:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Lagrangian}: Per-neuron Lagrangian FLOPs penalty with adaptive $\lambda$ updates, targeting 50\% FLOPs ratio. This is the standard approach critiqued in Section~\ref{sec:method}.
    \item \textbf{Magnitude pruning}: One-shot post-training pruning by weight $L_2$ norm, followed by fine-tuning.
    \item \textbf{Random structured pruning}: Fixed random mask applied before training, then frozen.
    \item \textbf{Linear schedule}: Linear sparsity ramp (instead of cubic) from step 500 to 4{,}000.
\end{itemize}

% ------------------------------------------------------------------
\subsection{Main Results}
\label{sec:main_results}

\paragraph{Lagrangian collapse confirms the diagnosis.} The central empirical result of this paper is the Lagrangian baseline's catastrophic failure on all three datasets (Table~\ref{tab:main}): per-neuron Lagrangian pruning catastrophically fails in elastic MoE architectures, achieving only $36.4\%$ on CIFAR-10, $10.4\%$ on CIFAR-100 (barely above the $1\%$ random baseline for 100 classes), and $4.7\%$ on Tiny-ImageNet. Despite reaching the target FLOPs ratio, the gradient-scale mismatch (Section~\ref{sec:method}) causes uniform mask collapse rather than selective pruning on every dataset tested. KaleidoNet avoids this failure mode entirely, with advantages of $45.6$, $48.7$, and $35.4$ percentage points on the three datasets respectively.

\paragraph{Schedule-based methods work.} The headline finding is that \emph{any} schedule-based pruning method---Linear, Random, Magnitude, or our cubic variant (KaleidoNet)---bypasses the gradient-scale failure mode and reaches near-dense accuracy on every dataset, in sharp contrast to the Lagrangian collapse. The ranking among schedules is consistent (KaleidoNet $>$ Linear $>$ Random $>$ Magnitude), but the spread is narrow: 81.0--82.0\% on CIFAR-10, 57.2--59.1\% on CIFAR-100, and 38.5--40.1\% on Tiny-ImageNet, all within 1--3 pp of the dense baseline. KaleidoNet's advantage over the linear schedule is 0.2--0.7 pp---modest but consistent (paired t-test: $p{=}0.07$ on CIFAR-100, $p{=}0.07$ on Tiny-ImageNet, $p{=}0.11$ on CIFAR-10; trending but underpowered with 3 seeds). KaleidoNet (cubic + gradient masking + dual-rate) is the best instantiation we tested, but the schedule shape is secondary to the diagnostic insight: what matters is that the budget signal is supplied externally rather than via gradient-based Lagrangian penalties at per-neuron granularity.

\paragraph{Convergence is critical.} Under the preliminary 5{,}000-step budget used in early experiments, all methods clustered around ${\sim}36\%$ accuracy on CIFAR-100 with ${\sim}5$ pp spread, and KaleidoNet trailed simpler baselines because the cubic schedule left insufficient recovery time after pruning completed. Training to convergence (50{,}000 steps) both raises all accuracies substantially and reverses the method rankings on all three datasets: the cubic schedule's gradual importance-based pruning now outperforms all pruning baselines, confirming that sufficient post-pruning recovery is critical for schedule-based methods.

\paragraph{Statistical significance.}
To assess robustness more carefully than a three-seed mean $\pm$ std permits,
we compute paired $t$-tests of every baseline against KaleidoNet across the
three matched random seeds, plus a 95\% bootstrap confidence interval on each
mean (Table~\ref{tab:main_stats}). The Lagrangian collapse is highly
significant on every dataset ($p<0.001$), confirming the gradient-scale
prediction. Magnitude and Random pruning are significantly worse than
KaleidoNet on at least two of three datasets ($p \leq 0.038$), and Linear
schedule trends in the same direction (CIFAR-100 $p=0.084$, Tiny-ImageNet
$p=0.068$, CIFAR-10 $p=0.106$) without crossing the conventional 0.05
threshold. With only three seeds these tests are underpowered; we therefore
treat $p < 0.05$ as indicative rather than confirmatory and recommend a
five-seed re-run for camera-ready. Cohen's $d$ and bootstrap CIs are reported
in the supplementary stats output.

\subsection{Accuracy--FLOPs Tradeoff}
\label{sec:pareto}

Figure~\ref{fig:pareto} visualizes the accuracy vs.\ active FLOPs tradeoff on CIFAR-100. The dense model achieves the highest accuracy (59.6\%) but at full FLOPs (236.7M). Among pruned methods operating at ${\sim}132$M FLOPs ($1.8{\times}$ reduction), KaleidoNet sits closest to the Pareto front, recovering 99.2\% of dense accuracy at 56\% of the computational cost. The gap between KaleidoNet and the next-best pruning method (linear schedule) is 0.7 pp, demonstrating that the cubic schedule's importance-aware pruning trajectory provides a meaningful advantage once training reaches convergence.

\subsection{Ablation Study}
\label{sec:ablation}

To understand the contribution of each architectural component, we train eight configurations on CIFAR-100 for 2{,}000 steps (Table~\ref{tab:ablation}). Each configuration enables or disables one of three pillars: \emph{elastic} (cubic schedule pruning), \emph{MoE} (expert routing), and \emph{early exit} (confidence-based).

\begin{tabular}{lccc}
\toprule

\midrule

At 2{,}000 steps, elastic pruning reduces accuracy by ${\sim}3.5$ pp because the cubic schedule is still actively pruning (at step 2{,}000 only 43\% of neurons are active, down from 100\%). However, with sufficient training (50{,}000 steps), the full KaleidoNet model recovers to $59.1\%$ on CIFAR-100, demonstrating that the accuracy cost of pruning is transient. MoE routing consistently adds ${\sim}0.4$ pp capacity, and early exit provides free regularization (+0.7 pp) without increasing FLOPs.

\paragraph{Key insight.} The cubic schedule requires sufficient training steps to demonstrate its value. At 2{,}000 steps, the simpler MoE + early exit combination dominates; at convergence (50{,}000 steps), KaleidoNet achieves the best accuracy--efficiency tradeoff among all pruning methods (Figure~\ref{fig:ablation}).

\paragraph{Schedule add-on ablation (converged budget).}
The pillar ablation above isolates KaleidoNet's \emph{architectural}
contributions (elastic / MoE / early exit). A separate question---raised
specifically by reviewers---is the contribution of the two
\emph{schedule add-ons} that distinguish KaleidoNet from a vanilla cubic
pruning baseline: \emph{irreversible gradient masking} and \emph{dual-rate
optimisation}. To isolate these, we run three ablation variants at the same
convergence budget as the main comparison (50{,}000 steps, three random
seeds, all three datasets):
\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Cubic only} --- cubic schedule with neither gradient masking
        nor dual-rate (mask logits share the main AdamW group; pruned logits
        can drift back).
  \item \textbf{Cubic + gradient masking, no dual-rate} --- once a neuron is
        removed by the schedule its mask logit is clamped to $-100$ and the
        gradient zeroed, but mask logits and weights still share a single
        learning rate.
  \item \textbf{Cubic + dual-rate, no gradient masking} --- mask logits use
        the higher mask LR ($3{\times}$ the task LR) but pruned logits are
        free to recover.
\end{itemize}
Together with the existing Linear-schedule baseline (linear ramp + both
add-ons) and KaleidoNet-full (cubic + both add-ons), these five conditions
isolate the marginal contribution of each schedule design choice at
convergence. Results are reported in Table~\ref{tab:ablation_schedule};
paired $t$-tests are computed against KaleidoNet-full across the three
matched seeds.

\paragraph{Interpretation: dual-rate optimisation is the only load-bearing add-on.}
The converged ablation (Table~\ref{tab:ablation_schedule}) sharply
decomposes the marginal contribution of each schedule add-on. Three
conclusions follow directly from the numbers.
First, \emph{cubic schedule alone} is meaningfully worse than the full
KaleidoNet on every dataset ($p\!\leq\!0.014$): the schedule by itself
reaches $80.4\%$/$56.6\%$/$36.4\%$ on CIFAR-10/100/Tiny-ImageNet,
roughly $1.7$--$3.7$ pp below KaleidoNet-full.
Second, \emph{adding gradient masking} to the cubic schedule produces
no significant improvement: $80.5\%$/$56.6\%$/$36.4\%$, statistically
indistinguishable from cubic-only. On Tiny-ImageNet the per-seed
trajectories of the gradient-masking variant are bit-identical to
cubic-only; on CIFAR-100 the mean differs by 0.03 pp, well within
seed noise. The hard-mask + zero-gradient mechanism, while motivated
by irreversibility, is in practice a no-op: by the time a logit is
clamped to $-100$ its gradient is already negligible, so explicitly
zeroing it changes nothing.
Third, \emph{adding dual-rate optimisation} to the cubic schedule
recovers KaleidoNet-full performance to within seed noise:
$82.05\%$/$59.12\%$/$40.09\%$ vs.\ KaleidoNet's
$82.02\%$/$59.09\%$/$40.09\%$ ($p\!\geq\!0.83$ on the harder benchmarks);
on Tiny-ImageNet the per-seed values are identical to four decimal
places. The dominant active ingredient of KaleidoNet is therefore the
$3{\times}$ mask-logit learning rate, which gives mask updates the
gradient scale they otherwise lack.
This decomposition reinforces the framing of the paper: the
\emph{diagnosis} of the gradient-scale failure mode is the primary
contribution, and the practical fix reduces, at convergence, to a
single hyperparameter (the mask-LR multiplier) that closes the
gradient-scale gap. The cubic schedule and the gradient-masking
mechanism are useful structural choices but are not the locus of the
gain.

\subsection{Computational Overhead}
\label{sec:overhead}

KaleidoNet's contribution is an \emph{accuracy--FLOPs tradeoff}, not a wall-clock speedup at current scale. While the $1.8{\times}$ FLOPs reduction represents genuine computational savings in active multiply-accumulate operations, MoE routing overhead dominates latency at this small model size. Table~\ref{tab:latency} reports inference latency benchmarks on Apple MPS (batch size 64).

\paragraph{Disclaimer (no deployment-speed claim).} We do not claim a deployment speedup at this scale. Wall-clock acceleration would require larger expert widths and CUDA-fused MoE kernels (e.g.\ MegaBlocks); the slowdown reported here is an implementation limitation, not a property of the algorithm.

KaleidoNet is approximately $18{\times}$ \emph{slower} than the dense ViT at inference on MPS---an implementation limitation we report transparently, not a claim or a property of the algorithm. This overhead arises because MoE routing (gating network, scatter/gather dispatching) dominates latency at this small model scale, far outweighing the savings from reduced per-expert compute. We conjecture (without claiming) that this gap would narrow at larger scales: at ViT-Large ($d{=}768$), per-expert FLOPs grow ${\sim}16{\times}$ while routing overhead remains approximately constant, which would suggest a smaller latency ratio; verifying this and benchmarking optimized MoE kernels (e.g., MegaBlocks~\citep{gale2023megablocks}) on CUDA hardware is left as future work and is not a claim of this paper. Training wall-clock time is similarly affected: the full KaleidoNet configuration (all pillars) takes $7{,}653$\,s for 2{,}000 steps compared to $967$\,s for the dense baseline (${\sim}8{\times}$ overhead), as shown in Table~\ref{tab:ablation}. Reducing MoE routing overhead at small scale is an important direction for future work.

\subsection{Pruning Trajectory}
\label{sec:trajectory}

Figure~\ref{fig:trajectory} shows the evolution of active neuron fraction over training. The cubic curve provides a smooth ramp from 100\% to 30\% active neurons between steps 500 and 4{,}000. Validation accuracy dips during the rapid pruning phase (steps 1{,}500--3{,}000) but recovers strongly after the schedule completes at step 4{,}000, eventually converging near 59\% by step 50{,}000. The long post-pruning recovery phase (steps 4{,}000--50{,}000) is essential: under the preliminary 5{,}000-step budget, KaleidoNet had not yet recovered from pruning and trailed simpler baselines; with sufficient convergence training, the cubic schedule's advantage fully materializes.

\section{Discussion and Conclusion}
\label{sec:discussion}

\paragraph{Summary.}
We identified the gradient-scale mismatch that causes Lagrangian FLOPs penalties to fail at per-neuron granularity in MoE architectures, and proposed KaleidoNet as a principled alternative. By replacing the learned penalty with a deterministic cubic sparsity schedule, gradient masking, and dual-rate optimization, KaleidoNet achieves stable selective pruning within expert sub-networks. In preliminary experiments with a limited 5{,}000-step training budget across four benchmarks, KaleidoNet delivers a consistent $1.8{\times}$ FLOPs reduction with 85--87\% accuracy retention. Under this short schedule, KaleidoNet does not yet outperform simpler pruning baselines (magnitude and random structured pruning), though the pruning trajectory analysis suggests the model has not converged and longer training may close this gap. Model surgery produces physically compact networks with $1.63{\times}$ parameter compression.

\paragraph{Limitations.}
Several limitations should be noted:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Training budget.} Our experiments use 5{,}000 steps (${\sim}$30 epochs on CIFAR-100), which is substantially shorter than typical ViT training schedules (300+ epochs). The accuracy gap between KaleidoNet and the dense baseline (4.86 pp on CIFAR-100) would likely narrow with longer training, as the pruning trajectory analysis shows the model has not converged.
    \item \textbf{Scale.} All experiments use small-scale datasets (32$\times$32 to 96$\times$96 pixels). Validation on ImageNet-1K would strengthen the generality claims but requires GPU cluster resources beyond our current setup.
    \item \textbf{Simple task parity.} On CIFAR-10 (10 classes), all pruning strategies---including random masking---achieve similar retention (${\sim}93$--$95\%$), suggesting that the structured pruning advantage of KaleidoNet is less pronounced when the classification task is simple. We expect differentiation to grow with task complexity and model scale.
    \item \textbf{Attention head pruning.} The cubic schedule currently targets only ElasticLinear neuron widths. All 6 attention heads survive pruning, leaving potential compression on the table.
\end{itemize}

\paragraph{Missing Lagrangian baseline.}
Although our method is motivated by the failure of Lagrangian FLOPs control at per-neuron granularity (Section~\ref{sec:gradient_analysis}), we do not include a direct comparison against a properly tuned Lagrangian baseline (e.g., with adaptive $\lambda$ scheduling, per-layer penalty scaling, or gradient normalization that might mitigate the scale mismatch). Our analysis demonstrates the failure mode analytically and empirically via the uniform collapse observation, but a controlled ablation comparing cubic scheduling against a well-tuned Lagrangian alternative at identical training budgets would strengthen the paper's central claim. We plan to address this gap in a future revision with Lagrangian baselines that use adaptive multiplier updates~\citep{lemaire2019structured} and per-layer FLOPs normalization.

\paragraph{Future work.}
Extending the cubic schedule to jointly prune attention heads and expert widths, scaling to ImageNet-1K with longer training, and investigating the interplay between expert count and pruning depth are promising research directions. The framework is also applicable to language models, where MoE layers are widely used.

\paragraph{Broader impact.}
Structured pruning methods such as KaleidoNet contribute to reducing the computational and energy footprint of neural network deployment. As vision transformers are increasingly adopted in real-world applications---from medical imaging to autonomous driving---the ability to compress models without specialized sparse hardware has direct implications for deployment on resource-constrained edge devices (mobile phones, embedded systems, IoT sensors). Smaller models require less memory, fewer FLOPs, and consequently less energy per inference, contributing to reduced carbon emissions at scale. Democratizing access to efficient models can also lower the barrier for researchers and practitioners in compute-limited settings. On the other hand, more efficient models may accelerate deployment in surveillance or other sensitive applications. We do not foresee negative societal impacts specific to our pruning methodology, but we encourage responsible deployment practices for any compressed model.

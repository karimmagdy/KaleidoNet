\begin{abstract}
\noindent
We show, with direct gradient measurements, why per-neuron Lagrangian
FLOPs pruning fails in Mixture-of-Experts (MoE) Vision Transformers:
the penalty never carries a per-neuron selection signal. The
ReLU-gated constraint emits zero gradient on more than $80\%$ of
training steps---switching off whenever the soft active fraction
reaches its target---and on the steps where it does fire it pushes
every logit in a layer equally. The optimiser therefore satisfies
the differentiable budget proxy without ever selecting neurons, a
failure we call \emph{selectivity collapse}: mask logits separate
under task gradient and sampling noise rather than importance,
entire layers can deactivate, executed compute drifts far from the
proxy ($61$--$81\%$ of the reference against a $50\%$ target), and
accuracy collapses to $36.4\%$, $10.4\%$, and $4.7\%$ on CIFAR-10,
CIFAR-100, and Tiny-ImageNet. The failure is robust: sweeping the
multiplier over three orders of magnitude under both fixed and
annealed Gumbel temperatures collapses in every configuration,
while every schedule-based method survives. As a practical remedy we propose
KaleidoNet, an instantiation that replaces the Lagrangian penalty
with a deterministic cubic sparsity schedule combined with dual-rate
optimisation---a $3\times$ higher mask-logit learning rate that our
ablation identifies as the active ingredient (an additional
irreversible gradient-masking safeguard proves inert at
convergence). Trained to convergence (50{,}000 steps, three seeds),
KaleidoNet achieves a $1.80\times$ active-FLOPs reduction and
retains $98.2$--$99.3\%$ of the unpruned reference accuracy, ranking
first among schedule-based pruning baselines on every dataset.
Post-training surgery yields a $1.63\times$ parameter compression.
Inference latency at this scale is dominated by MoE routing
overhead, a known limitation that shrinks at larger scale.
Validation covers CIFAR-10/100 and Tiny-ImageNet on a
${\approx}5.5$M-parameter MoE ViT; ImageNet-1K with larger backbones
is future work.
\end{abstract}

\section*{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}

Modern vision models increasingly rely on two architectural ideas that
trade raw parameter counts against conditional computation.
\emph{Vision Transformers} (ViTs)~\citep{dosovitskiy2021image} treat an
image as a sequence of patches and apply self-attention over them.
\emph{Mixture-of-Experts} (MoE) layers~\citep{shazeer2017outrageously,
fedus2022switch,riquelme2021scaling} replace a standard
feed-forward block with a bank of small sub-networks, called experts,
and a gating mechanism that sends each token to a small subset of
them (often only one). Together these ideas produce models whose
\emph{total} parameter count is large but whose \emph{active}
parameter count per input is small. This conditional computation is a
cornerstone of state-of-the-art language and vision models.

Even with MoE, the per-token active compute can be reduced further by
removing neurons \emph{inside} each expert. This is the domain of
structured pruning~\citep{han2015learning,li2017pruning,
liu2017learning,molchanov2017variational,chen2021chasing,yu2022width}. A widely adopted approach adds a
\emph{Lagrangian FLOPs penalty} to the training objective:
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:lagrangian}
\mathcal{L}_{\text{total}}
  = \mathcal{L}_{\text{task}}
    + \lambda\bigl(\mathrm{FLOPs}(\btheta) - B\bigr),
\end{equation}
where $B$ is a compute budget and $\lambda$ is a Lagrange multiplier
that can be learned or adapted online~\citep{gordon2018morphnet,
lemaire2019structured}. When each candidate structure carries a
differentiable mask, gradient descent is expected to simultaneously
minimise task loss and respect the budget, yielding a selective subset
of important components. This paradigm works well when the
granularity of decisions is coarse---e.g.\ pruning whole layers or
whole experts, where each decision controls $\sim\!10\%$ of total
FLOPs.

In this paper we study what happens when the same recipe is pushed to
the natural next level of granularity: pruning individual neurons
\emph{within} MoE experts. The intuition is that experts are
over-parameterised and that many of their hidden units are redundant
for a given task. Empirically, however, the Lagrangian recipe fails
catastrophically in this setting: it satisfies its differentiable
budget proxy yet converges to accuracy barely above random chance.

The contributions of this work are organised as follows.

\textit{Primary contribution---failure-mode diagnosis.} We show, by
measuring the task-loss and penalty gradient components on the mask
logits directly during training, that the per-neuron Lagrangian
penalty never functions as a selection signal. Two measured
properties are responsible. First, the penalty is \emph{gated}: with
the standard hinge formulation
$\lambda\,\mathrm{relu}(\hat{s}-s^{*})$ on the soft active fraction
$\hat{s}$, the gradient is exactly zero whenever the constraint is
satisfied in expectation---which, after a brief transient, is more
than $80\%$ of training steps at every multiplier setting we tested.
Second, when the gate is open the penalty is \emph{non-selective}:
its gradient on every mask logit of a layer is identical (each
neuron contributes equally to the layer's FLOPs), so it shifts all
logits in lock-step and carries no information about which neurons
matter. Mask logits therefore evolve under task gradient and
Gumbel-sampling noise alone, the executed compute decouples from the
soft proxy, and no task-aligned structure emerges---a failure we
call \emph{selectivity collapse}. This measured diagnosis is the
central novelty of the paper.

\textit{Secondary contribution---empirical validation across
baselines and configurations.} We confirm the diagnosis on three
datasets under a 50{,}000-step convergence budget: the Lagrangian
baseline collapses on every dataset while satisfying its soft budget
proxy (executed FLOPs drift to $61$--$81\%$ of the reference against
a $50\%$ target), and a probe grid over $\lambda \in [10^{-3},1]$
under both fixed and annealed Gumbel temperatures collapses in
every configuration. Every schedule-based pruning method (cubic,
linear, random, magnitude) survives, because it takes the
structural decision outside the penalty pathway entirely. The
defining axis is therefore not the specific schedule shape but
whether selection is delegated to a gradient that cannot carry it.

\textit{Tertiary contribution---KaleidoNet as a stable practical
instantiation.} We propose \textbf{KaleidoNet}, an elastic MoE ViT
that replaces the Lagrangian penalty with a deterministic
\emph{cubic sparsity schedule}~\citep{zhu2017prune} combined with
\emph{dual-rate optimisation}: mask logits are updated with a
learning rate three times larger than that of the task weights, so
structural decisions track the schedule faster than representations
drift. The convergence ablation (Table~\ref{tab:ablation_schedule}) shows
this is the only load-bearing add-on. A second mechanism,
\emph{gradient masking}---once a neuron has been removed by the
schedule, its mask logit is clamped to a large negative value and
its gradient is zeroed, so pruning decisions are irreversible---is
retained as a safeguard but is shown by the same ablation to be
inert at convergence. Together with a Gumbel-sigmoid reparameterisation and
straight-through estimator~\citep{jang2017categorical,
maddison2017concrete,bengio2013estimating,courbariaux2016binarized},
these choices let a simple importance heuristic (the mask logit
magnitude) drive selective pruning. The approach conceptually
inherits from earlier work on learnable gated networks and
structured regularisation~\citep{louizos2018learning} and on elastic
architectures~\citep{yu2019universally,cai2020once,hou2020dynabert};
see~\citep{chen2024dynamic} for a contemporary survey. KaleidoNet is
the best instantiation we tested, but the diagnostic insight is the
generalisable contribution.

Figure~\ref{fig:overview} summarises the setting, the two competing
pathways for the per-neuron pruning decision, and the three
contributions.

We evaluate the diagnosis and the fix on CIFAR-10, CIFAR-100, and
Tiny-ImageNet using a small but controlled four-block MoE ViT
(${\approx}5.5$M parameters). Under a shared 50{,}000-step convergence budget
with three seeds, the Lagrangian baseline collapses in the predicted
way (its gated penalty never expresses a preference between neurons,
so mask structure reflects noise rather than importance), while KaleidoNet's
schedule-based approach maintains selective pruning and achieves a
$1.80\times$ reduction in active FLOPs while retaining $98.2$--$99.3\%$
of the unpruned reference accuracy (Table~\ref{tab:main} and
Fig.~\ref{fig:pareto}). KaleidoNet attains $82.1\pm0.4\%$ on
CIFAR-10, $59.1\pm0.2\%$ on CIFAR-100, and $40.2\pm0.3\%$ on
Tiny-ImageNet, ranking first among schedule-based pruning baselines
on every dataset (cubic $>$ linear $>$ random $>$ magnitude, with a
0.2--0.7 pp margin over the next best method; the margin over the
linear schedule is not statistically conclusive at three seeds,
Table~\ref{tab:main_stats}). The residual gap to the
dense reference is small (0.4--0.9 pp) and reflects the unavoidable
cost of $1.80\times$ FLOPs compression. Post-training model surgery
physically removes pruned neurons and produces a compact network
with a $1.63\times$ parameter reduction. We frame these results as
contributions to FLOPs reduction and parameter compression rather
than to wall-clock acceleration: MoE routing overhead dominates
wall-clock inference at this scale, so KaleidoNet is $\sim\!18\times$
slower than the dense ViT on Apple MPS---a documented
platform-specific implementation limitation rather than a claim of
this work, expected to shrink substantially at larger model scales.

The honest takeaway is that the Lagrangian--MoE pruning recipe, which
has been assumed to scale to any granularity, in fact breaks at
per-neuron level inside experts. Schedule-based pruning with a
properly scaled mask learning rate is a simple, stable alternative, and the analysis extends
naturally to other architectures where decisions are made at a
granularity too fine for budget gradients to be informative.

\section*{Results}
\label{sec:results}

\subsection*{Experimental setup}

All experiments use a small but controlled four-block MoE ViT with
embedding dimension $d{=}192$, six attention heads, four MoE experts
with top-1 routing, and per-neuron elastic layers in each expert.
Patch sizes are 4 for CIFAR-10/100 ($32{\times}32$ images) and 8 for
Tiny-ImageNet~\citep{le2015tiny} ($64{\times}64$ images downsampled
to $32{\times}32$); we follow the data-efficient ViT training recipe
of~\citep{touvron2021training}. The reference model for all training
comparisons---labelled \emph{Unpruned MoE (reference)} in the tables
and called the \emph{unpruned reference} in the text---is the
identical MoE backbone with elastic pruning disabled: all
${\approx}5.5$M parameters retained (4 experts $\times$ 2-layer FFN;
5.47--5.54M depending on the classification-head size), executing
239.5M per-token active FLOPs under top-1 routing. Because the
reference and every pruned model share the architecture, router, and
training recipe, the main comparison isolates the pruning mechanism
itself rather than an architecture difference. KaleidoNet trains the
same backbone with the cubic schedule enabled, carrying 2.18M active
parameters per token under top-1 routing. Throughout, \emph{active
FLOPs} denotes the per-token compute actually executed under top-1
routing (the meter used in every table); summing over all four
experts as if densely activated would instead give 682M FLOPs for
this backbone. We quote FLOPs reductions against the unpruned
reference's 239.5M on this meter. We train each model with batch size 64 for
$T{=}50{,}000$ steps (convergence budget), using
AdamW~\citep{loshchilov2019decoupled} for task parameters at learning
rate $3\times10^{-4}$ with cosine schedule (2{,}500-step warmup,
$5\%$ of the budget),
Adam for mask logits at learning rate $9\times10^{-4}$, gradient
clipping at norm 1.0, and three independent random seeds per
configuration. The cubic sparsity schedule window is a fixed
fraction of the training budget---pruning begins at $10\%$ and ends
at $80\%$ of max steps, i.e.\ steps 5{,}000 to 40{,}000 at the
convergence budget---with target sparsity 70\%, leaving a long post-schedule
recovery phase for the task parameters to re-adapt to the pruned
architecture. Unless otherwise noted, all quantities report mean
$\pm$ standard deviation across three seeds.

\paragraph{Apples-to-apples baseline configuration.} All methods in
Table~\ref{tab:main} (KaleidoNet, Linear, Random, Magnitude,
Lagrangian) use the \emph{identical} early-exit confidence-head
architecture, ponder regulariser $\lambda_{\mathrm{ponder}}{=}0.01$,
exit threshold $\theta{=}0.95$, and MoE balance-loss coefficient
$\beta_{\mathrm{bal}}{=}0.01$; the primary axis that varies between
rows is the per-neuron mask-training rule (cubic vs.\ linear vs.\
random vs.\ magnitude vs.\ Lagrangian). One protocol difference is
disclosed for completeness: schedule-based methods anneal the
Gumbel-sigmoid temperature from $1.0$ to $0.1$ over the first
2{,}500 steps, whereas the Lagrangian family runs at its fixed
initialisation $\tau{=}5.0$; the Lagrangian sensitivity analysis
(Discussion) covers temperature explicitly, so the collapse is not
an artefact of this choice. This rules out the concern
that the Lagrangian baseline's collapse could be attributed to a
mis-configured early-exit head or balance-loss coupling rather than
to the gated, non-selective penalty we diagnose.

\subsection*{The gated penalty in practice}

Figure~\ref{fig:mechanism} shows the mechanism measured directly
during training. Separating the two gradient sources on the mask
logits by independent backward passes (Methods, measurement
protocol), the task-loss component holds steady at
${\sim}2\times10^{-6}$ per logit, while the penalty component
spikes only during the first few hundred steps---while dual ascent
is still raising $\lambda$ and the soft active fraction $\hat{s}$
still exceeds the target---and is \emph{exactly zero} thereafter
(84\% of all steps for the baseline configuration;
Fig.~\ref{fig:mechanism}a). The dual variable plateaus once
$\hat{s}$ settles at the target (Fig.~\ref{fig:mechanism}c): the
constraint is satisfied in expectation, the hinge gate closes, and
from that point the penalty exerts no influence whatsoever on which
neurons survive. Mask structure is left to the task gradient and
Gumbel-sampling noise.

The failure is insensitive to configuration. Across a probe grid of
$\lambda_0 \in \{10^{-3},10^{-2},10^{-1},1\}$ under both the fixed
($\tau{=}5.0$) and annealed ($1.0\!\to\!0.1$) temperature protocols,
accuracy at the probe budget lands between $3.5\%$ and $3.8\%$ in
every configuration, while a cubic-schedule run at the identical
budget reaches $30.2\%$ (Fig.~\ref{fig:mechanism}b). The gate-closed
fraction is $80$--$88\%$ in every cell of the grid.

Over the full 50{,}000-step runs the consequences accumulate
(Fig.~\ref{fig:collapse}): per-layer active fractions disperse from
the target rather than converging on it---some layers deactivate
entirely, which by itself is catastrophic for a four-block
network---and the final mask logits show wide within-layer spread
that reflects noise rather than task-aligned selectivity. The
executed per-token compute of the converged models ends at
$61$--$81\%$ of the unpruned reference (against the $50\%$ target)
across all nine runs, even though the soft proxy sat at its target
throughout: the differentiable proxy and the executed compute
decouple. The network pays the accuracy price of heavy stochastic
masking with none of the intended compression.

\subsection*{Main comparison: pruning methods at a 1.80$\times$ FLOPs budget}

We compare four pruning methods applied to the same elastic MoE
backbone, plus the unpruned MoE reference, across CIFAR-10, CIFAR-100,
and Tiny-ImageNet. Schedule-based methods share the cubic schedule's
start and end steps but differ in the sparsity curve they use. The
\emph{linear} baseline replaces the cubic ramp with a linear one.
The \emph{random} baseline fixes a uniformly random binary mask of
the target sparsity before training and freezes it. The
\emph{magnitude} baseline trains the dense network, then prunes once
by $L_2$ norm to the target sparsity, then fine-tunes for the
remainder of the budget. The \emph{Lagrangian} baseline follows
Eq.~\ref{eq:lagrangian} with per-neuron gradients, as derived in the
Methods. KaleidoNet uses the cubic schedule with dual-rate
optimisation (and the gradient-masking safeguard).

Table~\ref{tab:main} reports the $1.80\times$ FLOPs operating point of
the accuracy--compression trade-off for all five methods; the full
sweep at FLOPs ratios $\{1.3, 1.5, 1.8, 2.0, 2.5\}\times$ is reported
in Figure~\ref{fig:pareto}, and
sensitivity to schedule hyperparameters (mask-LR multiplier, schedule
shape) is reported in the same sensitivity analysis below. At the
$1.80\times$ point, KaleidoNet is the best pruning method on all three
datasets, attaining $82.1\pm0.4\%$ on CIFAR-10, $59.1\pm0.2\%$ on
CIFAR-100, and $40.2\pm0.3\%$ on Tiny-ImageNet (Figure~\ref{fig:pareto}
also visualises the point estimates). This retains $98.2$--$99.3\%$ of
dense-model accuracy (gap of only 0.4--0.9 pp) at ${\sim}55\%$ of the
compute cost. The ranking among schedule-based methods is consistent
across every dataset: KaleidoNet (cubic) $>$ linear $>$ random $>$
magnitude, with margins of 0.2--0.7 pp over the next-best baseline
(paired $t$-test: $p{=}0.07$ on CIFAR-100, $p{=}0.07$ on
Tiny-ImageNet, $p{=}0.11$ on CIFAR-10---trending but underpowered
with three seeds). The Lagrangian baseline collapses as predicted
($36.4\pm1.2\%$ on CIFAR-10, $10.4\pm0.3\%$ on CIFAR-100,
$4.7\pm0.3\%$ on Tiny-ImageNet) despite reaching the same FLOPs
target, representing accuracy gaps of $46.7$, $49.2$, and $36.1$ pp
relative to the dense reference.

\paragraph{Statistical significance.}
A natural concern with small-sample experiments is whether observed
mean differences carry statistical weight. All schedule-based
methods and the unpruned reference were therefore run with five
matched random seeds, and Table~\ref{tab:main_stats} reports paired
$t$-tests of every baseline against KaleidoNet across those seeds
(the collapsed Lagrangian-family baselines retain their original
three seeds; their verdicts are unambiguous at any sample size).
The Lagrangian collapse is highly significant on every dataset
($p<0.001$), directly confirming the selectivity-collapse
prediction. Magnitude and Random pruning are significantly worse
than KaleidoNet on all three datasets ($p \leq 0.006$). The
KaleidoNet-vs-Linear margin is significant on the two harder
datasets---CIFAR-100 ($t(4){=}5.70$, $p{=}0.005$) and Tiny-ImageNet
($t(4){=}5.17$, $p{=}0.007$)---and remains inconclusive on CIFAR-10
($t(4){=}2.48$, $p{=}0.068$); we word our ranking claims
accordingly. Relative to the unpruned MoE reference, the
$1.80\times$ compression costs a significant but small amount of
accuracy on CIFAR-10 and Tiny-ImageNet ($0.7$--$0.9$ pp) and a
non-significant amount on CIFAR-100 ($p{=}0.115$).

Three observations should be stated clearly.

\textit{Every schedule-based method bypasses the gated penalty;
KaleidoNet is the best instantiation tested.} The decisive
axis in Table~\ref{tab:main} is not which schedule shape is used but
whether the structural decision is made \emph{outside} the penalty
pathway. All four schedule-based
methods (cubic, linear, random, magnitude) reach near-dense accuracy
under the same $1.80\times$ FLOPs budget---each one recovers
$93.6$--$99.3\%$ of the reference accuracy across datasets---while the
Lagrangian baseline collapses on every dataset while satisfying
only its soft budget proxy (its executed compute drifts to
$61$--$81\%$ of the reference). This is the empirical signature of the diagnosis:
the failure mode is specific to optimising the per-neuron decision
through a budget gradient, and \emph{any} mechanism that takes the
decision out of that gradient pathway recovers near-dense accuracy.
Among the schedule-based methods, the ranking is consistent across
datasets (cubic $>$ linear $>$ random $>$ magnitude), and KaleidoNet
(cubic + masking + dual-rate) is the best instantiation we tested;
the residual gap to the dense reference (0.4--0.9 pp) reflects the
unavoidable cost of compressing the network to ${\sim}55\%$ of its
original FLOPs budget. The schedule shape is therefore secondary to
the diagnostic insight: practitioners should adopt schedule-based
pruning over Lagrangian pruning at this granularity, with KaleidoNet
as a stable default.

\textit{At this scale, non-MoE ViT controls outperform the MoE
backbone itself.} Two controls requested by the review isolate the
architecture from the pruning strategy (Table~\ref{tab:main}, second
block): a plain dense ViT of the same width (no experts, no masks;
175.2M per-token FLOPs) and a thin dense ViT calibrated to
KaleidoNet's executed budget (134.4M). Both outperform not only
KaleidoNet but the \emph{unpruned} MoE on all three datasets---the
FLOPs-matched thin ViT reaches $85.3\%$/$60.2\%$/$43.4\%$ against
KaleidoNet's $82.0\%$/$59.1\%$/$40.1\%$ at essentially the same
executed compute. We state the implication plainly: at
${\approx}5.5$M-parameter scale on $32{\times}32$ inputs, the MoE
architecture is an accuracy liability rather than an asset, with or
without pruning, and a practitioner whose goal is simply the best
model at a given FLOPs budget should train a thin dense ViT. This
does not affect the paper's diagnostic contribution---which concerns
\emph{how pruning decisions interact with penalty gradients inside
an MoE}, whatever the reason for using one---and it does not affect
the within-MoE comparisons, but it bounds the practical relevance
of pruned small-scale MoEs and we report it as such. Conditional
capacity is expected to pay off only at scales where experts can
specialise~\citep{fedus2022switch,riquelme2021scaling}; whether the
crossover interacts with pruning is an open question for the
larger-scale follow-up already discussed.

\textit{Convergence is essential for schedule-based methods.}
Preliminary 5{,}000-step runs showed all schedule-based methods
clustered around ${\sim}36\%$ on CIFAR-100 with a ${\sim}5$ pp
spread, and KaleidoNet trailed simpler baselines because the cubic
schedule left insufficient recovery time after pruning completed at
step 4{,}000. Training to convergence (50{,}000 steps) both raises
absolute accuracies and reverses the method ranking: once the network
has had time to re-adapt to the pruned architecture, the cubic
schedule's importance-aware trajectory ranks ahead of all alternatives.
This point is revisited in the pillar ablation below, which is run at
2{,}000 steps and therefore mid-schedule.

\subsection*{Pruning trajectory}

Figure~\ref{fig:trajectory} shows the evolution of the \emph{elastic
active fraction} (the fraction of expert-FFN neurons kept by the
schedule) and the validation accuracy during training. At the
convergence budget the pruning window spans steps 5{,}000 to

Eq.~\ref{eq:cubic} drives the elastic active fraction from $100\%$
to $30\%$ with the steepest removal in the first third of the
window. Accuracy at this budget climbs throughout training, with
only a brief plateau early in the pruning window
(${\sim}$steps 7{,}500--8{,}000, where capacity is being removed
fastest), and converges to ${\sim}59\%$ on CIFAR-100 over the final
${\sim}10{,}000$ steps. The contrast with the preliminary
5{,}000-step budget (schedule window 500--4{,}000) is instructive:
there, accuracy visibly dipped during pruning and the network had
not recovered by the end of training, so KaleidoNet trailed simpler
baselines; with the convergence budget the post-schedule recovery
phase is long and the cubic schedule's advantage fully materialises.

\subsection*{Ablation of the three architectural pillars}

cubic schedule, \emph{MoE} routing, and \emph{early exit} via
confidence heads. To separate their contributions, we train eight
configurations that enable or disable each pillar independently on
CIFAR-100 for 2{,}000 steps with a single seed (Table~\ref{tab:ablation}).
At this shorter budget, elastic pruning actively hurts because the
cubic schedule is still in its rapid-pruning phase (active fraction
$\sim\!43\%$ at step 2{,}000); the MoE + early-exit combination is
the best performer. At the full 50{,}000-step convergence budget
used in the main comparison, the full KaleidoNet model recovers to
$59.1\%$ on CIFAR-100, ranking first among the pruning methods and
trailing the dense baseline by only 0.5 pp. The ablation thus
isolates the cost of pruning (elastic) as \emph{transient}, resolved
by the long post-schedule recovery phase at convergence, while the
benefits of MoE and early exit are complementary and
budget-independent.

\subsection*{Schedule add-on ablation at convergence}

The pillar ablation above isolates KaleidoNet's \emph{architectural}
contributions (elastic / MoE / early exit). A separate question is
the contribution of the two \emph{schedule add-ons}
that distinguish KaleidoNet from a vanilla cubic pruning baseline:
irreversible gradient masking and dual-rate optimisation. To isolate
these we run three ablation conditions at the same convergence budget
as the main comparison (50{,}000 steps, three seeds, all three datasets):
(a) cubic schedule with neither add-on (mask logits share the main
AdamW group; pruned logits can drift back); (b) cubic with gradient
masking but a single learning rate; (c) cubic with dual-rate but no
gradient masking. Together with the linear-schedule and KaleidoNet-full
conditions from Table~\ref{tab:main}, these five conditions isolate the
marginal contribution of each schedule design choice
(Table~\ref{tab:ablation_schedule}). Paired $t$-tests are computed
against KaleidoNet-full across the three matched seeds.

\input{stats_ablation}

\paragraph{Dual-rate optimisation is the only load-bearing add-on.}
The converged ablation (Table~\ref{tab:ablation_schedule})
decomposes the marginal contribution of each schedule add-on at the
50{,}000-step convergence budget. \emph{Cubic schedule alone} is
significantly worse than the full KaleidoNet on every dataset
($p\!\leq\!0.014$), reaching $80.4\%$/$56.6\%$/$36.4\%$ on
CIFAR-10/100/Tiny-ImageNet, roughly $1.7$--$3.7$ pp below KaleidoNet.
Adding \emph{gradient masking} to the cubic schedule produces no
detectable improvement ($80.5\%$/$56.6\%$/$36.4\%$): on Tiny-ImageNet
the per-seed trajectories of the gradient-masking variant are
bit-identical to cubic-only, and on CIFAR-100 the mean differs by
$0.03$ pp---well within seed noise. The hard-mask + zero-gradient
mechanism, while motivated by irreversibility, is in practice a
no-op: by the time a logit is clamped to $-100$ its gradient is
already negligible, so explicitly zeroing it changes nothing.
Adding \emph{dual-rate optimisation} to the cubic schedule, by
contrast, recovers KaleidoNet-full to within seed noise:
$82.05\%$/$59.12\%$/$40.09\%$ versus KaleidoNet's
$82.02\%$/$59.09\%$/$40.09\%$ ($p\!\geq\!0.83$ on the harder
benchmarks; per-seed values are identical to four decimal places on
Tiny-ImageNet). The dominant active ingredient of KaleidoNet is
therefore the $3{\times}$ mask-logit learning rate, which lets the
task-driven importance signal move the logits decisively before the
schedule commits each pruning decision. This decomposition
reinforces the broader framing of the paper: the \emph{diagnosis} of
the gated, non-selective penalty is the primary contribution, and
the practical remedy reduces, at convergence, to a single
hyperparameter (the mask-LR multiplier) applied inside a mechanism
that never asks the penalty to select. The \emph{loss-scale} row
of Table~\ref{tab:ablation_schedule} closes the natural follow-up
question: replacing the higher mask learning rate with an equivalent
$3\times$ scaling of the mask-logit gradient (at the base learning
rate) reproduces cubic-only almost exactly on all three datasets
($80.7\%$/$56.4\%$/$36.5\%$), exactly as the Adam-invariance
argument in the Methods predicts---the lever is the
post-normalisation update magnitude, which only the learning rate
controls.

\subsection*{Model surgery and compression}

(keep neuron $i$ if mask logit $m_i \geq 0$) and physically removes
the corresponding rows and columns from every elastic weight matrix.

network with reduced per-expert widths. Table~\ref{tab:surgery}

Outputs are identical, within numerical precision, to the masked model.

\subsection*{Wall-clock latency}

We report wall-clock inference latency on Apple MPS (batch size 64,
100 iterations after a warm-up of 20 iterations) in
Table~\ref{tab:latency}. KaleidoNet is approximately $18\times$
\emph{slower} than the dense ViT at this scale. The overhead arises
from MoE routing (the gating network and scatter/gather operations
that assemble per-expert token lists), which does not scale with
per-expert compute. Because our expert FFNs are small
($4d{=}768$ hidden units pruned to $\sim\!230$), the routing cost
dominates. At larger scale---e.g.\ ViT-Large with $d{=}768$ and FFN
widths of $4d{=}3{,}072$---per-expert compute grows by roughly
$16\times$ while routing overhead remains approximately constant,
suggesting the ratio would shrink to $\sim\!2{-}3\times$. Optimised
MoE kernels (e.g.\ MegaBlocks~\citep{gale2023megablocks}) would
further reduce overhead. This limitation is reported upfront as a
matter of honest accounting: KaleidoNet is a method for FLOPs
reduction, not a drop-in speedup at the tested scale.

\section*{Discussion}
\label{sec:discussion}

\paragraph{What the diagnosis says and does not say.}
Our main claim is diagnostic, and it is measured rather than
asserted: the per-neuron penalty is gated off for the vast majority
of training and is non-selective within a layer when active
(Fig.~\ref{fig:mechanism}), so no choice of $\lambda$---we measured
three orders of magnitude---produces selectivity. The diagnosis
\emph{does not} imply Lagrangian pruning is broken at coarser
granularities: when the units being pruned control different
amounts of compute (whole layers, whole experts), the penalty
gradient differs across units and can express a preference, and
Lagrangian methods work well there~\citep{gordon2018morphnet,
lemaire2019structured}. The practical guide: use Lagrangian
penalties where pruning units have heterogeneous cost; use
schedule-based or importance-based pruning where the units are
interchangeable.

\paragraph{A Lagrangian sensitivity analysis.}
To probe whether the failure is about $\lambda$ alone, we ran an
instrumented probe grid of the \emph{global-$\lambda$} baseline:
initial multipliers $\lambda_0 \in \{10^{-3},10^{-2},10^{-1},1\}$
under both the fixed temperature ($\tau{=}5.0$) and the annealed
protocol ($\tau\!:1.0\!\to\!0.1$ over 2{,}500 steps), at a
5{,}000-step probe budget on CIFAR-100 with gradient components
logged throughout (Fig.~\ref{fig:mechanism}b and Methods,
measurement protocol). Every configuration collapses to
$3.5$--$3.8\%$ accuracy with the penalty gate closed on
$80$--$88\%$ of steps, while a cubic-schedule run at the identical
budget trains normally; no configuration produced selective
pruning. To
probe whether the failure is about $\lambda$'s
\emph{shape} (global vs.\ per-layer vs.\ per-expert) rather than its
magnitude, we additionally evaluated four strengthened-Lagrangian
variants on the same MoE-ViT backbone, training schedule, and
target FLOPs ratio: per-layer $\lambda_\ell$, per-(block, expert)
$\lambda_{e,\ell}$, augmented Lagrangian (with quadratic violation
term $\tfrac{\mu}{2}\,(\mathrm{FLOPs}-B)^2$, $\mu{=}0.1$), and
explicit FLOPs-gradient rescaling (FLOPs gradient multiplied by
$3\times$ to match the dual-rate factor used by the cubic-schedule
method). Table~\ref{tab:lagrangian_strengthened} reports the
results across CIFAR-10 and CIFAR-100. The per-expert variant
attains $36.17 \pm 2.65\%$ on CIFAR-10 and $10.12 \pm 0.24\%$ on
CIFAR-100 (only $\approx 9$~pp above random for a 100-class
problem), with its executed compute decoupled from the target
(satisfying only the soft proxy, as in the global baseline). This
confirms the diagnosis: reshaping $\lambda$ at per-layer or
per-expert granularity does \emph{not} eliminate the mismatch at
\emph{per-neuron} granularity within an expert. The effective
per-neuron penalty is $\lambda \cdot \mathrm{flops}_i/B$ regardless
of how $\lambda$ is parametrised, so the resulting collapse mode
persists. Strikingly, all four strengthened-Lagrangian variants
collapse to within $\pm 1$~pp of each other \emph{and of the
global-$\lambda$ baseline} on CIFAR-100:
per-layer $9.37 \pm 0.89\%$, per-expert $10.12 \pm 0.24\%$,
augmented $10.08 \pm 0.16\%$, rescaled $9.71 \pm 0.26\%$, against
$10.45 \pm 0.33\%$ for the global baseline
(Table~\ref{tab:lagrangian_strengthened}). The reason follows
directly from the diagnosis: reshaping the dual variable changes
\emph{how strongly} the constraint is enforced per layer or per
expert, but leaves the within-expert cost symmetry untouched---no
shape of $\lambda$ lets the penalty prefer one neuron over another
inside an expert. The augmented-Lagrangian quadratic term and the
explicit penalty-gradient $3\times$ rescale do not change the
qualitative outcome either, and in every variant the executed
compute decouples from the target exactly as in the global
baseline. The diagnosis is therefore strengthened, not weakened, by
the variant sweep: \emph{no} reshaping of the Lagrangian dual
variable recovers selectivity at per-neuron granularity within an
expert.

Because the variants' executed compute never matched the target,
one could still ask whether they fail merely by operating at a
harder compression level. The bottom block of
Table~\ref{tab:lagrangian_strengthened} closes this question: we
re-ran the cubic schedule with its final sparsity calibrated so
that its executed per-token compute lands exactly in the range the
Lagrangian variants reached ($64$--$75\%$ of the reference). At
these matched budgets the schedule loses nothing ($82.8$--$83.1\%$
on CIFAR-10, $59.7$--$59.9\%$ on CIFAR-100), while every Lagrangian
variant at the same executed compute remains collapsed---and the
schedule's \emph{standard} operating point is more compressed than
any variant reached. The gap is therefore attributable to
selectivity, not to compression quantity.

\paragraph{Schedule-window sensitivity.}
The window parameters of the cubic schedule are not delicately
tuned. A one-factor-at-a-time star around the operating point
($t_0{=}500$, $t_1{=}4{,}000$), run at the full 50{,}000-step budget
on CIFAR-100 within a matched single-seed harness, varies accuracy
by less than one percentage point: $t_0 \in \{250, 1{,}000,
2{,}000\}$ gives $57.7$/$57.8$/$57.6\%$ and $t_1 \in \{2{,}000,
8{,}000, 16{,}000\}$ gives $57.5$/$57.3$/$58.3\%$, against $57.4\%$
for the unperturbed configuration in the same harness ($59.1\%$ in
the main-table protocol; the $\approx\!1.6$ pp offset between the
two harnesses is constant across configurations and does not affect
the within-star comparison). The long post-schedule recovery phase
absorbs even an $8\times$ change in either window parameter. The
final-sparsity axis is covered by the matched-budget runs of
Table~\ref{tab:lagrangian_strengthened}, which train the schedule at
$s_f \in \{0.39, 0.55, 0.70\}$ with smooth, small accuracy
variation. These are single-seed probes and we label them as such.

The L0 / Hard-Concrete baseline\cite{louizos2018learning}, which
sidesteps the FLOPs-budget formulation entirely by penalising the
expected $L_0$ norm of the mask, exhibits a similar but
mechanistically distinct collapse: it over-compresses relative to
the target (executed per-token FLOPs ${\approx}35\%$ of the
unpruned reference rather than the targeted $50\%$), attains
$37.86 \pm 0.47\%$ on CIFAR-10 (slightly above the
strengthened-Lagrangian variants), but collapses to $9.91 \pm 0.15\%$
on CIFAR-100 like all the others. The shared failure across both
formulations indicates that the issue is not specific to a
Lagrangian penalty---it is a property of any per-neuron
sparsity-inducing mechanism that asks a rate-style differentiable
penalty, indifferent between interchangeable neurons, to perform
selection at MoE-expert granularity. Schedule-based methods bypass
this by removing the structural decision from the gradient path
entirely (Methods).

\paragraph{What is novel.}
The primary novelty of this work is the measured failure-mode
diagnosis: the per-neuron Lagrangian penalty is gated to zero for
the vast majority of training and is non-selective between
interchangeable neurons when active, so it cannot perform selection
inside MoE experts regardless of multiplier tuning---a property of
the granularity (cost symmetry within an expert) rather than of any
specific implementation. The empirical validation across four
schedule-based baselines, a $\lambda\times\tau$ probe grid, and
three datasets is the secondary contribution: every schedule-based
method (cubic, linear, random, magnitude) bypasses the penalty
pathway and reaches near-dense accuracy, while every Lagrangian
configuration collapses. The defining axis is therefore not the
schedule shape but whether selection is delegated to a gradient
that cannot carry it. KaleidoNet itself is a
tertiary contribution: a stable practical instantiation that
combines a cubic schedule with dual-rate optimisation (plus an
irreversibility safeguard that the ablation shows to be inert), and
that is the best instantiation tested in our experiments. The diagnostic insight is what generalises;
KaleidoNet is the recommended default given the diagnosis.

\paragraph{Scope: FLOPs vs.\ wall-clock.}
The contributions of this work concern \emph{FLOPs} and \emph{parameter
compression}, not wall-clock acceleration on production hardware.
These are distinct questions. FLOPs and active-parameter counts are
intrinsic properties of the pruned architecture and are reported here
as $1.80\times$ and $1.63\times$ reductions, respectively, on
schedule-based methods including KaleidoNet. Wall-clock latency, by
contrast, depends on hardware, kernel implementation, and routing
overhead; at the small scale we evaluate (${\approx}5.5$M-parameter MoE ViT,
expert FFN width $4d{=}768$ pruned to $\sim\!230$, Apple MPS), MoE
routing dominates inference time and KaleidoNet runs $\sim\!18\times$
slower than the dense ViT. We document this as a platform-specific
limitation rather than a counter-claim against the FLOPs reduction.
At larger expert widths and on CUDA with fused MoE
kernels~\citep{gale2023megablocks}, the routing fraction drops and
the FLOPs savings would translate into meaningful wall-clock gains;
that translation is not demonstrated here and is an obvious direction
for follow-up.

\paragraph{When is the accuracy--FLOPs tradeoff worthwhile?}
The tradeoff is worth taking whenever (a) active FLOPs or
active-parameter counts are hard-constrained---for example by an
energy or memory budget---(b) pruning is already on the
table, \emph{and} (c) the MoE architecture is already on the table
for reasons other than small-scale accuracy (conditional capacity,
modularity, or an existing MoE deployment): the ViT controls in
Table~\ref{tab:main} show that at this scale a plain thin ViT is
the stronger pure-accuracy choice at the same budget. Given
(a)--(c), KaleidoNet is the clear choice among the recipes tested,
and the residual gap to the unpruned reference is the cost of the
compression, not a failure of the method. It is less compelling when compute is
unconstrained, when the only metric is top-line accuracy, or when
wall-clock latency on small MoE kernels is the bottleneck
(Table~\ref{tab:latency} and the scope paragraph above). We
therefore frame the contribution as a targeted accuracy--FLOPs
design point with a clear set of use cases rather than a universal
win.

\paragraph{Relation to concurrent work.}
Several recent works address MoE compression from complementary
angles. SlimMoE~\citep{slimmoe2025} retains all experts and prunes
redundant neurons within each via iterative prune-and-distill. STUN
(structured-then-unstructured pruning)~\citep{stun2025} combines
expert-level and weight-level pruning for scalable MoE compression.
MoNE~\citep{mone2025} replaces redundant experts with lightweight
``novice'' networks. MoE-Pruner~\citep{moepruner2025} uses
router-guided magnitude pruning at the neuron level. Two further
works operate at exactly the granularity we analyse and merit
individual discussion. TENP~\citep{tenp2026} ranks experts by their
output magnitude and directional influence and then prunes
individual neurons inside the less important experts, preserving
parameters in a trapezoidal pattern across depth; notably, its
neuron-selection signal is an explicit importance score (projected
contribution to the expert output) computed \emph{outside} any
differentiable penalty---consistent with our diagnosis that a
budget gradient cannot carry this information. DERN~\citep{dern2025}
drops redundant experts using router statistics and then decomposes
them into neuron-level segments that are merged into the surviving
experts, again performing neuron-granular surgery retraining-free
and without a sparsity penalty. Both are post-hoc methods for
billion-parameter pretrained language MoEs, whereas we study
during-training pruning of a small vision MoE; the shared pattern
across settings is that successful per-neuron MoE compression
delegates selection to explicit importance signals rather than to
penalty gradients. Lu et
al.~\citep{lu2024expertslimming} show that not all experts contribute
equally and propose expert pruning and skipping for MoE LLMs. He et
al.~\citep{he2024demystifying} demystify MoE compression through a
unified framework, and Li et al.~\citep{li2024merge} exploit routing
policies to merge experts before compressing. Efficient expert
pruning for sparse MoE LMs~\citep{liu2024efficient} reports speedups
on larger architectures; upcycling pre-trained dense LMs into
MoEs~\citep{he2025upcycling} addresses the opposite problem of
turning dense models sparse. MaskLLM~\citep{fang2024maskllm} shows
that learnable semi-structured sparsity with Gumbel-softmax sampling
can scale to billion-parameter language models, and mask-conditional
compression for ViTs~\citep{zhou2025mcnc} applies related ideas to
non-MoE vision transformers. Prior representation-collapse
analyses~\citep{chi2022representation} and expert-pruning
works~\citep{kim2023mixture} focus on expert-level decisions. Our
contribution is orthogonal to all of these: we characterise
\emph{why} Lagrangian approaches fail at per-neuron granularity
inside MoE, which informs the design of these and future methods
regardless of their specific pruning strategy.

\paragraph{Relation to head-level / differentiable sparsity literature.}
Our diagnosis sits inside a broader literature on differentiable
sparsity and head-level gating that is worth naming explicitly.
\textbf{L0 / Hard-Concrete} regularisation~\citep{louizos2018learning}
parameterises the mask via a stretched concrete distribution with a
differentiable surrogate to the $\ell_0$ norm; the resulting penalty
is on the \emph{rate} of active masks rather than on FLOPs, which
sidesteps the $\mathrm{flops}_i/B$ scale mismatch entirely. We
evaluate L0 / Hard-Concrete as a head-to-head baseline in
Table~\ref{tab:main} and Table~\ref{tab:lagrangian_strengthened}.
\textbf{Stochastic Gates} (STG)~\citep{yamada2020stg} use a Bernoulli
relaxation with a separate $\ell_2$ regulariser on the active rate,
which is closely related to L0 in spirit and stands as a natural
second differentiable-sparsity baseline for future comparison. \textbf{MorphNet}~\citep{gordon2018morphnet}
applies a Lagrangian penalty at \emph{per-layer} granularity --- exactly
the regime where, by our analysis, the penalty can still express a
preference (different layers control different amounts of compute);
our per-layer-$\lambda$ strengthened-Lagrangian variant
(Table~\ref{tab:lagrangian_strengthened}) is the natural per-neuron
analogue. \textbf{Augmented Lagrangian / ADMM}
methods~\citep{boyd2011admm,zhang2018admm,bertsekas1996constrained,
nocedal2006numerical} add a quadratic penalty term to soften the
constraint enforcement; we evaluate the augmented variant as a
strengthened-Lagrangian condition. The takeaway is that the
selection failure we diagnose is specific to \emph{per-neuron}
budget-style penalties; changing granularity (per-layer /
per-expert) restores the penalty's ability to discriminate between
units, whereas replacing the FLOPs constraint with an
$\ell_0$/active-rate surrogate does not (the surrogate is equally
indifferent between interchangeable neurons, and collapses in our
experiments as well).

\paragraph{Limitations.}
Our experiments use a small model ($\sim\!5$M parameters) trained on
three $32{\times}32$-resolution datasets (CIFAR-10, CIFAR-100,
Tiny-ImageNet) with a shared 50{,}000-step convergence budget; this
choice isolates the pruning mechanism across all methods but limits
the strength of the scale claim. The conjecture that selectivity
collapse persists at ImageNet-1K with larger ViTs is structural
rather than scale-dependent---the hinge gate and the within-expert
cost symmetry that disable the penalty's selection capacity do not
depend on width or dataset size---but it is not validated
empirically.
Attention-head pruning is not addressed by the current schedule: all
six heads survive in KaleidoNet, leaving potential compression on
the table. Expert-utilisation dynamics (routing balance under
pruning, and whether the router compensates for pruned experts) are
not analysed in depth. The extension of the diagnosis to
language-model MoEs (Switch, GShard) is an explicit conjecture with
no empirical evidence in this paper. Wall-clock latency at this
scale is $18\times$ that of the dense baseline due to MoE routing
overhead, a limitation that, while expected to shrink at larger
scale, cannot be read off our experiments directly; no deployment
speedup is demonstrated. In practical terms, the method is
best suited to settings where active FLOPs or parameters are
hard-constrained; practitioners whose bottleneck is small-batch
latency on current hardware, or who need post-hoc compression of an
already-trained model, are better served by the post-hoc
neuron-level methods discussed under concurrent work. Finally, the
non-MoE controls show that at this scale the MoE backbone itself
underperforms a plain ViT at matched compute, so the practical
scope of pruned small-scale MoEs is limited to settings where the
MoE is required for other reasons; the diagnosis is independent of
this, but the instantiation's utility is not.

\paragraph{Future work.}
Three directions are natural extensions of this analysis. First,
applying the same schedule-based approach jointly to expert FFN
widths and attention heads, using a shared cubic curve or two
staggered ones, should close the attention-head compression gap.
Second, validating the gating analysis at ImageNet-1K scale
with ViT-Small or ViT-Base would turn the current conjecture into a
result. Third, the analysis may apply beyond vision: language-model
MoE layers have the same per-neuron cost symmetry, so we
conjecture---without empirical evidence in this paper---that the
gating diagnosis and the schedule-based fix transfer to pruning
expert FFNs inside models such as Switch
Transformer~\citep{fedus2022switch} or
GShard~\citep{lepikhin2021gshard}; testing this on a language MoE
is future work.

\section*{Methods}
\label{sec:methods}

\subsection*{Architecture}

KaleidoNet is a pre-norm Vision Transformer with four elastic
transformer blocks. Each block consists of an elastic multi-head
self-attention layer, a four-expert mixture-of-experts feed-forward
layer with top-1 routing, and a confidence head for early exit. We
describe each component in turn.

\textit{Elastic attention.} A standard multi-head self-attention layer
with $H{=}6$ heads and embedding dimension $d{=}192$. Each head has an
associated mask logit; during training the mask is sampled via a
Gumbel-sigmoid reparameterisation, and at inference it is a
deterministic hard threshold.

\textit{MoE feed-forward.} A Mixture-of-Experts layer with $E{=}4$
experts and top-1 routing. A linear gating network
$\bw_g\in\R^{d\times E}$ selects the highest-scoring expert per
token~\citep{fedus2022switch}. Each expert is a two-layer FFN:
\begin{equation}
\mathrm{FFN}_e(\bx)
  = \mathrm{ElasticLinear}_2\bigl(\mathrm{GELU}\bigl(
       \mathrm{ElasticLinear}_1(\bx)\bigr)\bigr),
\end{equation}
with $\mathrm{ElasticLinear}_1:\R^d\to\R^{4d}$ and
$\mathrm{ElasticLinear}_2:\R^{4d}\to\R^d$.

\textit{Elastic linear layer.} Given input $\bx\in\R^{d_{\mathrm{in}}}$,
mask logits $\bm\in\R^{d_{\mathrm{out}}}$, weights $W$, and bias $b$,
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:elastic}
\mathrm{ElasticLinear}(\bx)
  = (W\bx + b)\odot\mathrm{GumbelSigmoid}(\bm,\tau),
\end{equation}
where $\odot$ is elementwise multiplication and $\tau$ is a
temperature. A minimum-active constraint keeps at least one neuron
active per layer to prevent degenerate solutions.

\textit{Confidence head.} Each block produces a scalar confidence
score via a linear layer followed by a sigmoid. During inference the
score gates an optional early exit; during training the associated
ponder cost acts as a mild regulariser.

\subsection*{Why Lagrangian per-neuron pruning fails}

We analyse the penalty exactly as implemented and then verify each
step of the analysis with direct gradient measurements during
training. The compute-constrained recipe (Eq.~\ref{eq:lagrangian})
is implemented, as is standard for differentiable pruning, on a
\emph{soft budget proxy}: the mean soft mask across the $M$ elastic
neurons of the model,
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:proxy}
\hat{s}(\bm) = \frac{1}{M}\sum_{i=1}^{M} \sigma(m_i/\tau),
\qquad
\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{FLOPs}}
 = \lambda\,\mathrm{relu}\!\bigl(\hat{s}(\bm) - s^{*}\bigr),
\end{equation}
where $s^{*}$ is the target active fraction (0.5 in our
experiments) and $\sigma$ the sigmoid. Differentiating,
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:gradscale}
\frac{\partial \mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{FLOPs}}}{\partial m_i}
 = \underbrace{\mathbb{1}\bigl[\hat{s} > s^{*}\bigr]}_{\text{gate}}
   \cdot \lambda \cdot
   \underbrace{\frac{\sigma'(m_i/\tau)}{M\,\tau}}_{\text{per-neuron factor}}.
\end{equation}
Two structural properties follow, and both are confirmed by
measurement (Fig.~\ref{fig:mechanism}).

\emph{Step 1 --- the penalty is gated.} The hinge makes the entire
penalty gradient exactly zero whenever the soft proxy satisfies the
constraint. Measured on the mask logits during training (task and
penalty components separated by independent backward passes), the
penalty gradient is identically zero on $80$--$88\%$ of steps for
every $\lambda \in \{10^{-3},10^{-2},10^{-1},1\}$ and under both
temperature protocols: after a transient of a few hundred steps,
dual ascent raises $\lambda$ until $\hat{s}$ settles at $s^{*}$ and
the gate closes (Fig.~\ref{fig:mechanism}a,c). From that point the
penalty exerts no influence on which neurons survive.

\emph{Step 2 --- when the gate is open, the penalty is
non-selective.} Within a layer, every neuron contributes the same
amount to $\hat{s}$, so the open-gate gradient in
Eq.~\ref{eq:gradscale} differs across neurons only through
$\sigma'(m_i/\tau)$, which is maximal (and nearly equal) precisely
for the undecided logits near zero. The penalty therefore shifts all
undecided logits of a layer in lock-step: it can reduce the proxy,
but it cannot express a preference between neurons. Selection is
left entirely to the task gradient and the Gumbel-sampling noise.

\emph{Step 3 --- the consequences.} Because the proxy $\hat{s}$ is
soft, satisfying it does not pin down the executed compute: hard
active fractions per layer drift apart (some layers deactivate
completely), and the executed per-token FLOPs of the converged
models end at $61$--$81\%$ of the unpruned reference against the
$50\%$ target across all nine runs, even though the soft proxy sits
at its target throughout (Fig.~\ref{fig:mechanism}c,
Fig.~\ref{fig:collapse}). The result is \emph{selectivity collapse}:
masks whose structure reflects noise rather than importance, with
catastrophic accuracy (Table~\ref{tab:main}).

\begin{remark}
This failure mode is specific to fine granularity. At per-layer or
per-expert granularity, the units being pruned control
\emph{different} amounts of compute, so the penalty gradient differs
across units and can express a preference between them; per-layer
Lagrangian methods work well in this
regime~\citep{gordon2018morphnet}. At per-neuron granularity within
an expert, all units are interchangeable in cost, and the penalty's
selection capacity vanishes by symmetry.
\end{remark}

\paragraph{Why adaptive optimisation does not rescue the penalty.}
A natural objection is that Adam-style preconditioning normalises
update magnitudes and might restore the penalty's influence. It
cannot, for two measured reasons. First, no rescaling can revive a
gradient that is exactly zero: the gate of Eq.~\ref{eq:gradscale}
closes for the vast majority of steps, and Adam's second-moment
estimate merely adapts to the surviving task-gradient component.
Second, Adam normalises the \emph{summed} per-coordinate gradient
and is blind to its composition: on the steps where the gate opens,
the penalty component is (near-)identical across a layer's
coordinates, so preconditioning rescales it together with the task
component and preserves its non-selectivity. The same argument
explains why rescaling the penalty term itself is a no-op: under
Adam, multiplying a loss term by a constant $c$ scales the first
moment by $c$ and the square root of the second moment by $c$ as
well, leaving the applied update approximately invariant. Our
$\lambda$-grid measurement confirms this directly---multipliers
spanning three orders of magnitude produce indistinguishable
collapse (Fig.~\ref{fig:mechanism}b)---and the same invariance
predicts that dual-rate optimisation cannot be replaced by a loss
or gradient scaling factor, which we verify as a separate ablation
in Table~\ref{tab:ablation_schedule}.

\paragraph{Measurement protocol.} All gradient-component
measurements use 5{,}000-step probes on CIFAR-100 (seed 1,
batch 64): every 100 steps we run two additional backward
passes on the current batch---task loss only, then penalty only---
and record the mean absolute gradient per elastic layer, together
with the Adam second-moment state of the mask logits, the dual
variable $\lambda$, and the soft active fraction $\hat{s}$. The
probe grid covers $\lambda_0 \in \{10^{-3},10^{-2},10^{-1},1\}$
under (i) the Lagrangian family's fixed $\tau{=}5.0$ and (ii) the
schedule methods' annealed $\tau$ ($1.0\!\to\!0.1$ over 2{,}500
steps); a cubic-schedule run at the identical probe budget provides
the reference. The 50{,}000-step mask-distribution time series
(logged every 500 steps for all convergence runs) provide the
long-horizon trajectories in Fig.~\ref{fig:collapse}.

\paragraph{Implementation details of the Lagrangian baseline.} The
Lagrangian baseline reported in Table~\ref{tab:main} mirrors the
standard per-neuron recipe so that the failure we diagnose is
attributable to the gated, non-selective penalty of
Eq.~\ref{eq:gradscale} rather than to under-tuning. Specifically:
(i) a single shared scalar multiplier $\lambda$ initialised at
$0.01$, updated by dual ascent at $\eta_{\mathrm{dual}}{=}10^{-2}$
on the normalised violation (the soft active fraction minus the
target ratio) and clamped to $[0,100]$ (implementation in
\texttt{experiments/baselines/lagrangian\_pruning.py});
(ii) AdamW for task parameters at LR $3\times 10^{-4}$, separate
Adam for mask logits at $9\times 10^{-4}$ ($3\times$ multiplier ---
the same dual-rate factor used by the cubic-schedule method, so
that the comparison is on the structural-decision rule rather than
on the optimiser); gradient clip $1.0$;
(iii) Gumbel-sigmoid temperature fixed at its initialisation
$\tau{=}5.0$ for the Lagrangian family (schedule-based methods
instead anneal $\tau$ from $1.0$ to $0.1$ over the first 2{,}500
steps; the temperature sensitivity analysis in the Discussion
verifies the collapse is not an artefact of this difference);
(iv) no per-layer / per-expert reparameterisation in this baseline
\emph{by design} --- this is the underpowered configuration whose
failure we diagnose. Strengthened variants (per-layer
$\lambda_\ell$, per-expert $\lambda_{e,\ell}$, augmented
Lagrangian, penalty-gradient rescaling) are evaluated separately
in Table~\ref{tab:lagrangian_strengthened} and discussed in the
sensitivity analysis (Discussion).

This analysis concerns FLOPs and parameter granularity; wall-clock
latency on production hardware is a separate question, addressed
below as a documented limitation rather than a claim of this work.

\subsection*{Cubic sparsity schedule and mask optimisation}

The cubic sparsity schedule~\citep{zhu2017prune} externally dictates
the target active fraction at each training step, bypassing the
penalty pathway entirely. Given start step $t_0$, end step
$t_1$, and final sparsity $s_f$,
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:cubic}
s(t) =
\begin{cases}
0 & t < t_0, \\[2pt]
s_f\,\Bigl[1 - \bigl(1 - \tfrac{t - t_0}{t_1 - t_0}\bigr)^3\Bigr]
   & t_0 \leq t \leq t_1, \\[2pt]
s_f & t > t_1.
\end{cases}
\end{equation}
We set the window as fixed fractions of the training budget:
$t_0$ at $10\%$ and $t_1$ at $80\%$ of max steps ($t_0{=}5000$,
$t_1{=}40{,}000$ under the 50{,}000-step convergence budget;
$t_0{=}500$, $t_1{=}4000$ under the preliminary 5{,}000-step
budget), with $s_f{=}0.70$. The cubic shape
removes neurons fastest at the start of the pruning window---after
the $t_0$ warm-up has established initial importance estimates, while
representations are still plastic enough to absorb the loss of
capacity---and decelerates smoothly toward $t_1$, so the final
decisions are made gently and the surviving subnetwork is stable
well before the schedule ends. The post-schedule phase (the final
$20\%$ of training) then provides recovery at full architectural
stability.

\textit{Gradient masking.} Every 100 steps, for each elastic linear
layer, the $k = \lfloor s(t)\cdot d_{\mathrm{out}}\rfloor$ neurons
with the lowest mask logits are \emph{permanently} deactivated:
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:gradmask}
m_i \leftarrow -100,
\qquad
\frac{\partial\mathcal{L}}{\partial m_i} \leftarrow 0,
\quad
\text{for pruned neurons}.
\end{equation}
Setting $m_i = -100$ ensures $\sigma(m_i/\tau) \approx 0$ for any
$\tau$, and zeroing the gradient prevents accidental recovery. Hard
masking makes decisions irreversible by construction; the
convergence ablation (Table~\ref{tab:ablation_schedule}) shows it is in
practice a safeguard rather than an active ingredient---by the time
a logit is clamped to $-100$ its gradient is already negligible.
What drives selectivity is the schedule itself: rather than hoping
weak Lagrangian gradients will find the right pattern, the schedule
\emph{forces} the pattern at each step, using the mask logit as a
task-driven importance score whose updates are given adequate scale
by the dual-rate optimisation below.

\textit{Gumbel-sigmoid with straight-through.} For the active
neurons, the forward pass samples a binary mask via
\begin{equation}
\hat{m}_i = \mathbb{1}\bigl[\sigma\bigl((m_i + g_1 - g_2)/\tau\bigr)
 > 0.5\bigr],
\end{equation}
where $g_1, g_2 \sim \mathrm{Gumbel}(0,1)$ and the indicator uses the
straight-through estimator~\citep{bengio2013estimating} (hard forward,
soft backward). For schedule-based methods the temperature $\tau$
anneals linearly from $1.0$ to $0.1$ over the first 2{,}500 steps,
transitioning from stochastic exploration to near-deterministic
masks early and holding $\tau{=}0.1$ for the remainder of training.

\textit{Dual-rate optimisation.} We split parameters into two
optimiser groups:
\begin{itemize}
  \item Task parameters (weights, biases, routing gates): AdamW
  with learning rate $3\times10^{-4}$, weight decay 0.01.
  \item Mask logits: Adam with learning rate $9\times10^{-4}$
  ($3\times$ higher), no weight decay.
\end{itemize}
Structural decisions must track the schedule faster than
representations drift; the higher mask learning rate supports this.

\subsection*{Training algorithm}

Algorithm~\ref{alg:training} summarises the training loop. The total
loss is
$\mathcal{L} = \mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{CE}} + 0.01\,\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{balance}}
 + 0.01\,\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{ponder}}$,
where $\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{CE}}$ is cross-entropy,
$\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{balance}}$ is the standard MoE load-balancing
loss~\citep{fedus2022switch}, and $\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{ponder}}$ is
the early-exit ponder cost. Importantly, \emph{no Lagrangian FLOPs
penalty is added}: the cubic schedule replaces it entirely.

\begin{algorithm}[t]
\caption{KaleidoNet training loop.}
\label{alg:training}
\begin{algorithmic}[1]
\REQUIRE Model $f_{\btheta}$ with elastic layers; data $\mathcal{D}$;
  total steps $T$; schedule $[t_0,t_1,s_f]$.
\STATE Initialise mask logits $\bm \leftarrow 0.5\cdot\mathbf{1}$.
\STATE Initialise optimisers: $\mathrm{opt}_w$ (lr $=3\mathrm{e}{-4}$),
  $\mathrm{opt}_m$ (lr $=9\mathrm{e}{-4}$).
\FOR{$t = 1,\dots,T$}
  \STATE Sample minibatch $(\bx,y) \sim \mathcal{D}$.
  \STATE $\tau \leftarrow 1.0 + (0.1 - 1.0)\cdot\min(t/2500,1)$.
  \STATE $\hat y \leftarrow f_{\btheta}(\bx;\bm,\tau)$.
  \STATE
   $\mathcal{L} \leftarrow \mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{CE}}(\hat y, y)
    + 0.01\,\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{balance}}
    + 0.01\,\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{ponder}}$.
  \STATE Backprop, clip gradients to norm 1.0.
  \STATE Update $\btheta$ via $\mathrm{opt}_w$; update $\bm$ via
    $\mathrm{opt}_m$.
  \IF{$t \geq t_0$ and $t \bmod 100 = 0$}
    \STATE Compute $s(t)$ via Eq.~\ref{eq:cubic}.
    \FOR{each elastic layer}
      \STATE $k \leftarrow \lfloor s(t)\cdot d_{\mathrm{out}}\rfloor$.
      \STATE Identify $k$ neurons with smallest $m_i$; set $m_i \leftarrow -100$,
        zero their gradient.
    \ENDFOR
  \ENDIF
\ENDFOR
\STATE \textbf{Model surgery:} drop rows/columns of pruned neurons.
\RETURN compact model $f_{\btheta'}$.
\end{algorithmic}
\end{algorithm}

\textit{Model surgery.} After training, soft masks are converted to
hard decisions (keep $i$ if $m_i\geq0$) and weight rows/columns of
removed neurons are physically deleted. The resulting model is a
standard dense network with reduced per-expert widths; no mask
overhead remains. If fewer than 50\% of neurons are active in a
layer, inference uses an \verb|index_select| sparse path; otherwise
it uses the dense path with masking.

\subsection*{Datasets and training details}

We use CIFAR-10 and CIFAR-100~\citep{krizhevsky2009learning} (10 and
100 classes respectively, $32{\times}32$ images, 50k train / 10k
test) and Tiny-ImageNet~\citep{le2015tiny} (200 classes,
$64{\times}64$ images downsampled to $32{\times}32$, 100k train / 10k
val). All models train for 50{,}000 steps with batch size 64 (three
seeds each), AdamW for task parameters with learning rate
$3\times10^{-4}$ and weight decay 0.01, Adam for mask logits with
learning rate $9\times10^{-4}$, cosine LR schedule with 500-step
warmup, gradient clipping at norm 1.0. The cubic sparsity schedule
window is fixed at $10\%$--$80\%$ of the training budget (steps
5{,}000 to 40{,}000 at 50{,}000 steps) with final sparsity 0.70. The
Gumbel-sigmoid temperature anneals linearly from 1.0 to 0.1 over the
first 2{,}500 steps for schedule-based methods (the Lagrangian family
runs at its fixed initialisation $\tau{=}5.0$). Balance
and ponder loss weights are 0.01. Hardware: Apple M4 (MPS backend)
with supplementary runs on Google Colab Pro (NVIDIA A100 / H100).

CIFAR-10 and CIFAR-100~\citep{krizhevsky2009learning} via the
Torchvision \verb|CIFAR10| / \verb|CIFAR100| loaders, and
Tiny-ImageNet~\citep{le2015tiny} from the Stanford CS231N
distribution. No proprietary data is used. The per-seed results
underlying Table~\ref{tab:main} are provided with the paper
repository as JSON files.

% =====================================================================
\section*{Code availability}
All code for training, ablation, model surgery, inference
benchmarking, and the instrumented gradient-component probes is
available at \url{https://github.com/karimmagdy/KaleidoNet},
released under an MIT license. The exact version used for this
study, including per-seed result files, is archived on Zenodo under
the version-specific DOI \texttt{10.5281/zenodo.21224908}. A reproduction
script trains the dense baseline and KaleidoNet with a single seed on
CIFAR-100 in under 30 minutes on an NVIDIA A100; the full three-seed
protocol across CIFAR-100 and Tiny-ImageNet runs in under 24 hours.
Key files and entry points are documented in the repository README.
